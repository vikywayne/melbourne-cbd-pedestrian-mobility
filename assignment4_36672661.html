Research Question

The primary research question for this project is:

How were Melbourne CBD pedestrian activity patterns associated with Victoria’s temporary free public transport initiative during April and May 2026?

To answer this question, the following sub-questions will also be explored:

- Did overall pedestrian activity change during the initiative period compared with February and March 2026?
- Which sensor locations experienced the largest changes in pedestrian activity?
- Did activity patterns vary across different times of day?

- Did pedestrian activity patterns vary by day of the week?

The 2026 datasets each contained 115 columns (Date, Hour and 113 sensor locations), while the 2025 datasets contained 108 columns. This indicated that additional sensors were introduced between 2025 and 2026. As a result, year-to-year comparisons required careful consideration of sensor availability to ensure like-for-like comparisons.

The audit also identified the use of non-standard missing value indicators (“na” and “undefined”) within the pedestrian count data. These values were converted to standard missing values during data cleaning to improve consistency and compatibility with downstream analysis.

The sensor metadata file contained 136 sensor records, including sensor descriptions, installation dates, operational status and geographic coordinates. This dataset was used to provide contextual information and support spatial visualisations.

The audit process informed several subsequent cleaning decisions, including the handling of missing values, selection of comparable sensors across years and preparation of data for spatial analysis.

Data processing

The following processing steps were undertaken:

1. Download monthly pedestrian count datasets and sensor location metadata.
2. Import data into R using the tidyverse packages.
3. Audit dataset structure and identify inconsistencies.
4. Convert non-standard missing values (“na” and “undefined”) into standard missing values.
5. Reshape the data from wide format into tidy long format.
6. Restrict year-to-year comparisons to sensors available in both 2025 and 2026.
7. Join pedestrian count data with sensor metadata where required.
8. Create aggregated datasets for temporal and spatial analysis.

Data processing involved combining monthly files, standardising missing values, restricting the analysis to sensors available in both years, and reshaping the data into a tidy long format suitable for analysis and visualisation. The resulting dataset contained one row for each sensor-hour observation and formed the basis for all subsequent analyses.

Motivation

Melbourne’s temporary free public transport initiative during April and May 2026 provided an opportunity to examine whether a transport policy intervention was associated with changes in pedestrian activity within the CBD. As someone who personally experienced the initiative, I was interested in understanding whether its effects could be observed in publicly available pedestrian movement data.

Beyond evaluating this specific initiative, the analysis also demonstrates how urban sensor data can be used to assess changes in city activity patterns. Insights from this type of analysis may help policymakers better understand how transport initiatives influence movement within the city and identify locations or periods that experience the greatest changes in activity.

Data

The primary dataset consists of hourly pedestrian counts collected from automated pedestrian sensors located throughout Melbourne CBD. Each observation represents the number of pedestrian movements recorded by a specific sensor during a particular hour.

The study focuses on six monthly datasets covering April–May 2025 and February–May 2026. These periods were selected to enable comparison of pedestrian activity before and during the free public transport initiative introduced in April 2026. A supplementary sensor metadata dataset was also used to provide information about sensor locations, installation details and operational status.

Analysis

Overall Pedestrian Activity Before and During the Initiative

Figure 1 shows daily pedestrian activity across Melbourne CBD from February to May 2026. A vertical dashed line marks the commencement of the temporary free public transport initiative in April 2026.

Pedestrian activity exhibited strong day-to-day variation throughout the study period. While daily counts remained relatively high following the introduction of the initiative, no immediate city-wide increase in pedestrian activity was visually apparent. This suggests that any association between the initiative and pedestrian activity may have been concentrated at specific locations or times of day rather than across the CBD as a whole.

Which Locations Experienced the Largest Changes?

Figure 2 shows the locations that experienced the largest changes in pedestrian activity between April–May 2025 and April–May 2026. The largest increases were observed at Flinders Lane–Swanston Street, Melbourne Central, RMIT Building 80 and Bourke Street Mall (South), all of which are located within highly active commercial, retail and university precincts. In contrast, several locations including Southbank, St Kilda Road–Alexandra Gardens and New Quay recorded lower pedestrian volumes during the same period. These findings suggest that changes in pedestrian activity were concentrated in specific areas of the CBD rather than being distributed evenly across all locations.

Did activity patterns vary across different times of day?

Figure 3 compares the average hourly pedestrian counts during April and May in 2025 and 2026. Both years exhibit a very similar daily pattern, with pedestrian activity remaining low overnight, increasing rapidly during the morning, remaining elevated throughout the afternoon, and reaching a peak during the late afternoon period.

Although the overall shape of the profiles is nearly identical, pedestrian activity in 2026 was generally higher during the evening hours. The largest differences occurred between 6 pm and 11 pm, where average pedestrian counts were consistently above the corresponding 2025 levels.

These results suggest that while the free public transport initiative was not associated with major changes in the overall daily pattern of movement within the Melbourne CBD, there may have been a modest increase in evening pedestrian activity. This finding is consistent with the possibility that free transport encouraged additional discretionary travel or extended visits to the city during evening periods. However, the observational nature of the data means that causation cannot be established.

Did Pedestrian Activity Patterns Vary by Day of the Week?

Figure 4 compares pedestrian activity by weekday and hour for April–May 2025 and April–May 2026. Activity remained concentrated during daytime and early evening hours across all days of the week. The similarity between the two heatmaps indicates that weekly behavioural patterns were largely unchanged between years. While some locations experienced increases in pedestrian activity, there was little evidence of substantial changes in the timing of pedestrian movements throughout the week.

Conclusions

The objective of this analysis was to investigate how Melbourne CBD pedestrian activity patterns were associated with Victoria’s temporary free public transport initiative during April and May 2026.

The results suggest that the initiative was not associated with a substantial city-wide increase in pedestrian activity. Daily pedestrian counts during the initiative period remained broadly consistent with patterns observed prior to its introduction. Similarly, hourly and weekly activity patterns were highly comparable between April–May 2025 and April–May 2026.

However, location-level analysis revealed that several key CBD locations experienced notable increases in pedestrian activity. Areas such as Melbourne Central, Flinders Lane–Swanston Street and Bourke Street Mall recorded substantially higher pedestrian volumes in 2026 compared with the equivalent period in 2025. This indicates that any changes in activity were concentrated within specific commercial, retail and transport precincts rather than being distributed evenly across the city.

Overall, the findings provide limited evidence that the free public transport initiative altered the broader temporal patterns of pedestrian movement within Melbourne CBD. While some locations experienced increased pedestrian activity, the observational nature of the data means that other factors such as events, tourism, economic conditions and seasonal variation may also have contributed to the observed changes.

Q6: What were the challenges encountered during the project ?

One challenge was that the results were not what I initially expected. Since the project focused on a free public transport initiative, I anticipated seeing a clear increase in pedestrian activity after its introduction. However, the city-wide trends showed only modest differences, requiring me to investigate activity at individual sensor locations and different time periods to better understand the patterns. Another challenge involved the sensor metadata. I initially planned to include more spatial analysis and map-based visualisations, but many sensor names in the pedestrian count data did not exactly match those in the metadata file. This limited the effectiveness of some mapping approaches and required additional checking and experimentation. I also spent time refining visualisations to ensure they contributed new insights rather than repeating information already shown in previous figures.

3) Tell us about any imperfect parts of your work and how you would like to expand or improve this analysis in future.

This analysis focused on a relatively short period consisting of six monthly datasets. While this was sufficient to compare pedestrian activity before and during the initiative, a longer time period would provide a stronger basis for identifying long-term trends and reducing the influence of short-term fluctuations. The analysis is also observational and cannot determine whether the free public transport initiative directly caused the changes observed in pedestrian activity. Other factors such as major events, tourism, weather conditions, university schedules and economic activity may also have influenced pedestrian movement throughout the CBD. If this project were extended, I would incorporate additional datasets and undertake more detailed spatial analysis to better understand how different areas of Melbourne responded to the initiative.
